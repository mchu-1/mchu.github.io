Human Artificial Chromosomes (HACs)

Towards a platform for genetic engineering in medicine

What do we need to genetically engineer humans?

Instead of making changes to our natural chromosomes, the way forwards is to pre-install an extra chromosome at birth via IVF. This lab-built 'mini-chromosome' acts as a DNA chip inside each cell. It is a modular upgrade slot where new functions can be installed, turned on or off, or swapped, while the native genome stays untouched.

What is a human artificial chromosome?

A HAC is built with the same basic parts as real chromosomes (a centromere for mitosis, telomeres at the ends), so it behaves like a small, separate chromosome rather than a piece of DNA that inserts into existing genes. In human cells, modern HACs are megabase-scale (about 0.5 to 10 Mb) and can carry large, well-regulated gene cassettes. Crucially, HACs can be maintained as autonomous, episomal chromosomes with low loss rates per cell division in tested lines.

Why should we pair HACs with IVF?

Doing this at the one-cell embryo stage means the scaffold is present in every tissue, not patchy later. IVF labs already perform delicate micro-manipulation (for example ICSI) and embryo micro-injection in research, showing the basic handling is feasible (even though clinical embryo editing is not allowed in most places). If HACs are ever used clinically, IVF is the natural place to install one standard slot once, then upgrade safely over a lifetime.

How could HACs be introduced into the embryo?

  • Prepare a compact, chromatinized HAC optimized for early replication and segregation.
  • Deliver by pronuclear micro-injection or co-injection during ICSI at the one-cell stage.
  • Culture to blastocyst, then verify HAC presence and copy number.
  • Transfer HAC-positive embryos; after birth the HAC segregates with native chromosomes so the scaffold is present across tissues.

Why does a shared scaffold matter?

As uses multiply (repairs, sensors, interfaces, performance), a common scaffold avoids each program reinventing promoters, insulators, landing pads, and safety rules. The HAC can host pre-wired tissue-specific docking sites (cardiac, hepatic, neural, immune, and others) so payloads run where they should, on their own schedules, without crosstalk. Mechanistically, HACs often use satellite DNA with CENP-B boxes to build a functional centromere, and newer HACs may even bypass canonical centromeric DNA.

How does one receive genetic upgrades over time?

Once the scaffold exists, site-specific integrases or recombinases (for example Bxb1, PhiC31, Cre/lox, and Flp/FRT) and CRISPR-based editors can add, update, or remove cassettes precisely at the HAC docking sites. These systems already work in mammalian cells and are being engineered for higher efficiency and specificity. Small upgrades can be delivered later by tissue-targeted carriers or nanoparticles, since only the inserts must travel, not the entire chromosome.

How could health be monitored continuously with a HAC?

HACs could carry molecular recorders that write time-stamped information into DNA as cells experience events. In the nearer term, cell-free RNA in blood already offers a window into what tissues are doing. Future HAC programs could standardize such readouts for longitudinal, minimally invasive monitoring.

Conclusion

With IVF + HAC, we get a clean separation of concerns for the future of genetic medicine. The native genome stays untouched, and complex functionality lives on a modular, maintainable synthetic episome.
